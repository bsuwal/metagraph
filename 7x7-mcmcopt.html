Random samples versus demonstration plans

Up until now, we've focused on understanding a "normal range" for the parties among samples of districting plans that are drawn in a neutral way — by an MCMC random walk. Here, we wil shift the focus to instead find plans to demonstrate what is possible rather than what is normal For instance, we might want to know the largest number of districts that can be drawn competitively in a plan without sacrificing other rules. Or if we are checking compliance with the Voting Rights Act, we might want to know how many districts can be simultaneously drawn with a certain group in the majority. We'll consider how to make demonstration plans of different kinds on the 7x7 grid.

Finding extremes

The first thing we'll do is use the MCMC sampler we built in the last demo , but instead of using it to generate an ensemble of plans to compare against, we'll use it to hunt for plans which award a high number of seats to one of the parties. We've slightly modified the code to look for extreme outcomes. Once you pick your distribution and click to search, the algorithm will run for 1,500 steps in short bursts of 150. Every 150 steps, it restarts its walk from the most extreme plan it has found so far, and at the end it displays the best one found overall.

The idea behind this is that plans which disproportionately favor one particular party tend to be near each other in the metagraph, so we should search for improvements near the best plan we've already seen. However, we don't want to get stuck in a neighborhood which has pretty good plans, but not the best, so we introduce some randomness to get us unstuck, in a way that is moderated by the restart frequency.

You'll notice that this does really well, but not always perfectly. For example, if you make the top three rows all Clubs and the bottom four all Hearts, if you try a few times, the algorithm will probably find a plan that lets the Hearts Party win six seats, but almost certainly not one with seven Hearts districts. This is for two reasons — first, that there is only one plan which has seven Hearts-favoring districts (seven vertical strips), and the second is that this plan has very low degree in the metagraph, so it's hard for the random walk to find it. It's remarkable that it can even find a plan with six Hearts-favoring districts. If you plug that distribution into the sampling tool on the previous page and look at the histograms, you'll see that the vast majority of plans have four Hearts seats, with a small handful of plans with five. Very rarely will it even find a six-Hearts-seat plan, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the restarting mechanism.

This is using the same MCMC algorithm as before, it just remembers the most extreme plan it's seen so far. If we step back for a second, this is a pretty ineffective way of constructing a gerrymander. If someone decides to pick an unfair map by clicking the MCMC button which generates the most extreme map possible, then it will be very easy for us to use the same MCMC algorithm to show that the plan is an extreme outlier.

Elections aren't a sure thing

Of course in real life, gerrymanders are harder to detect than this because of the uncertainty in elections. If there is some randomness in the outcomes and the Hearts Party wins six of the seven seats, is that because the Clubs Party is structurally disadvantaged by the districting plan or were they just unlucky this time? We'll incorporate this into our model and show how we can still use the MCMC sampler to help us answer this question.

Electoral uncertainty

Before we move forward, we need an additional piece of machinery from probability theory. Up until now, we've treated elections as deterministic, meaning that a party will surely win a seat if at least half of the squares in the corresponding district are of that color. Now, we'll introduce a little bit of uncertainty. To keep the math simple, we'll now say that the probability that a party wins a seat depends on the number of squares in the coresponding district of that color. We'll use the following rule to decide the probability that the Hearts Party wins a seat:

Number of Hearts Squares Chance the Hearts Party Wins
0 Hearts Never
1 Hearts Never
2 Hearts 10%
3 Hearts 33%
4 Hearts 66%
5 Hearts 90%
6 Hearts Always
7 Hearts Always

The rule is the same for the Clubs Party.

Now, instead of talking about how many seats a particular party wins, we need to talk about how many and with what probability. The districting plan is fixed, and as you change the voters, you'll see the probability that the Hearts Party wins each district as well as total probabilities for the Hearts Party winning any particular number of seats. Since we only have two parties, this also tells us the same information for the Clubs Party's prospects, since, for example, the probability that the Hearts Party wins three seats is exactly the same as the probability that the Clubs Party wins four seats.

Again, this does pretty well, though not always perfectly. One reason for this is that ten rounds of 150 steps is not all that many samples, compared to the total universe of plans. Additionally, instead of simply winning a district by having the majority of its voters be Hearts, we care about how many Hearts voters are in each district. To put it more concretely, in the old model, there was no difference between having a $\frac{4}{7}$ Hearts and a $\frac{6}{7}$ Hearts district and two $\frac{5}{7}$ Hearts districts, but now we care about this distinction. We've gone from eight possible outcomes, Hearts winning zero to seven districts, to over 3,000, the number of possible probability distributions over these randomized outcomes.

You'll also notice that, in general, as you pick larger numbers, the histogram spreads out more. This is because, as long as there isn't an overwhelming majority of Hearts voters, a plan which gives Hearts a shot at six or seven seats needs to have the Hearts voters spread out across many districts, whereas plans which only need to secure one or two seats can safely pack many Hearts voters into a few districts. The natural context in which to analyze this tradeoff is to look at the competitiveness and representativeness of plans.

Demonstrating Representativeness and Competitiveness

We first need to articulate definitions of these concepts before analyzing any plans. The representativeness of a plan measures how close the final division of seats matches the proportions of Hearts and Clubs voters in the population. A plan in which the voters are approximately half Hearts and half Clubs but very rarely awards more than two Hearts seats is far from representative, whereas a plan which almost always has either three or four Hearts seats is highly representative. In terms of the histograms, from the previous two demos, we can think of representativeness as how tightly the bars are clustered around the value of $k$ which is closest to the true fraction of Hearts voters.

Competitiveness is a little more subtle. First, we'll call a district "competitive" if it's close to having an equal chance of being won by Hearts or Clubs. Formally, we'll measure it by the difference between $50\%$ and the percent chance that the Hearts Party wins it (remember, since the probabilities are symmetric we could just as well choose to measure against the Clubs Party's chances). In 7x7 Gridlandia, the competitive districts are the ones which have three voters from one party and four from the other. We can then measure the competitiveness of a plan by taking the total over all of the districts. A perfectly competitive plan (which doesn't exist here, because of odd numbers) gets a score of zero. With respect to the histograms, competitveness describes how spread out the bars are. A more competitive plan allows for a wider variety of outcomes, so the histogram bars will be spread out more, and a less competitive plan will have a narrow, spiked histogram.

This should immediately raise an alarm. Representativeness and competitiveness both sound like good things, but they also sound like they're incompatible with each other. If there are 14 Hearts voters all in two districts and 35 Clubs voters all in the remaining five, then the outcome of the election will always be perfectly representative and extremely non-competitive. Using the same numbers of voters, we could draw every district with two Hearts voters, resulting in a plan which is a little more competitive, but much less representative. But, this is all in the abstract, as whether such a plan even can be drawn depends on where the Hearts voters are actually located in Gridlandia. If they aren't arranged in a suitable way, either of these kind of plans might be impossible to find. Given an arrangement of voters, we might be interested in what the most competitive or representative plan is, and using MCMC to find demonstration plans is a great way to find such things.

Finding a demonstration plan which shows that, for example, there exists a set of districts which offers proportional representation for the Clubs Party relates very strongly to how gerrymandering is challenged in real life. If some group comes forward alleging that it is systematically disenfranchised by the enacted districting plan, being able to use a demonstration plan which shows that there is some viable alternative which does not disadvantage them is very compelling evidence both in courts of law and in the court of public opinion. Since the universe of districting plans for the 7x7 grid, let alone a proper juridiction, is far too large to search exhaustively, we can use MCMC to guide our metagraph exploration.

Over the last two chapters, we've introduced two facets of MCMC for redistricting which, even in the simple example of small grids, are incredibly powerful tools for the analysis of gerrymandering. Previously, we looked at using MCMC for outlier analysis, where we let the MCMC algorithm generate a big sample of plans, called an ensemble, and then compared our plan of interest to the outcomes across the ensemble. This provides us with statistical evidence, since we can detect if a plan yields outcomes which are too extreme relative to the ensemble. Here, we've used MCMC to search a plan meeting some criterion, such as maximal representativeness or competitiveness. But, we can notice that these two things aren't very different, we've just used a different method for crawling around on the metagraph. Before, when we were looking for a random sample, we walked around randomly. Here, since we're looking for something in particular, we modify the MCMC process to more directly move toward what want. There are of course many other ways we could walk around the metagraph. The scientific community, including MGGG, are thinking hard about these different ways of walking around the metagraph both in theory and in practice. Since the metagraph for a real jurisdiction is so complex, it's useful to have more basic testbeds, like the 7x7 grid, to experiment with these concepts.
