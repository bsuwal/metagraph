Finding extremes

The first thing we'll do is use the MCMC sampler we built in the last demo, but instead of using it to generate an ensemble of plans to compare against, we'll use it to hunt for plans which award a high number of seats for one of the parties. We've slightly modified the code to look for extreme outcomes. Once you pick your distribution and click to search, the algorithm will run for 1,500 steps in short bursts of 150. Every 150 steps, it restarts its walk from the most extreme plan it has found so far, and at the end it displays the best one found overall.

The idea behind this is that plans which disproportionately favor one particular party tend to be near each other in the metagraph, so we should search for improvements near the best plan we've already seen. However, we don't want to get stuck in a neighborhood which has pretty good plans, but not the best, so we introduce some randomness to get us unstuck, in a way that is moderated by the restart frequency.

You'll notice that this does really well, but not always perfectly. For example, if you make the top three rows all Clubs and the bottom four all Hearts, if you try a few times, the algorithm will probably find a plan that lets the Hearts Party win six seats, but almost certainly not one with seven Hearts districts. This is for two reasons -- first, that there is only one plan which has seven Hearts-favoring districts (seven vertical strips), and the second is that this plan has very low degree in the metagraph, so it's hard for the random walk to find it. It's remarkable that it can even find a plan with six Hearts-favoring districts. If you plug that distribution into the sampling tool above and look at the histograms, you'll see that the vast majority of plans have four Hearts seats, with a small handful of plans with five. Very rarely will it even find a six-Hearts-seat plan, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the restarting mechanism.

Electoral uncertainty

Before we move forward, we need an additional piece of machinery from probability theory. Up until now, we've treated elections as deterministic, meaning that a party will surely win a seat if at least half of the squares in the corresponding district are of that color. Now, we'll introduce a little bit of uncertainty. To keep the math simple, we'll now say that the probability that a party wins a seat is equal to the proportion of squares in the coresponding district of that color. In this new framework, we say that a district with three Hearts and four Clubs squares will be won by the Clubs Party with probability $\tfrac{4}{7}$ and the Hearts Party with probability $\tfrac{3}{7}$.

Now, instead of talking about how many seats we can get for the Hearts Party, we need to talk about how many and with what probability. Below, you can adjust the arrangement of the voters to play around with this concept. The districting plan is fixed, and as you change the voters, you'll see the probability that the Hearts Party wins each district as well as total probabilities for the Hearts Party winning any particular number of seats.

From the perspective of the Hearts Party, these probabilities are incredibly important. Notice that if you make each district have four Hearts voters and three Clubs voters, then there is still a reasonable probability of the Clubs Party winning a majority of seats, but if you have four districts with seven Hearts voters, and three with seven Clubs voters, then the Clubs Party can never win a majority of the seats.

Now, the key problem to consider is supposing we want at least a certain number of the districts to be won by the Hearts Party with a certain probability, how should we draw the districts? We've introduced a new degree of freedom, called seat fragility which quantifies how secure the Hearts Party feels about the outcome. Using the example above, we might feel better about drawing a plan where the Hearts Party is guaranteed to win four of the seven seats than one which leaves the door open for our rivals to win a majority, even if it does allow the possibility of winning a larger majority sometimes.

The quantity we're interested in examining is the cumulative probability of the Hearts Party winning at least some number of seats $k$. In other words, we can take any districting plan and arrangement of voters and figure out the probability of there being at least $k$ Hearts Party wins in our new randomized model. As we just saw, the plan which maximizes this quantity is different for different choices of $k$. The safe plan, where four districts have all Hearts voters and three have all Clubs voters is the best choice if we choose $k$ to be four, but it's definitely not the right one if we want $k$ to be five.

This all seems well and good, but even once we pick $k$, we've made the problem of finding the best plan much harder, at least by hand. Before, we could kind of rely on a packing-and-cracking heurstic where we try to make the Clubs Party win their districts by a bigger margin than the Hearts Party wins theirs, but that doesn't seem like quite enough when we're working in this richer probabilistic model. Fortunately for us, the MCMC algorithm from before is fantastic at performing this search.

The algorithm is the same as before, where it performs a random walk and occasionally restarts, keeping track of the best one it found so far. The two charts on the right show the probabilities we're interested in. The first one is the probability of the Hearts Party winning exactly that number of seats, and the one on the bottom is the cumulative probability of the Hearts Party winning at least that number of seats.
